Part 1

Colonialism: Definition and Causes

What colonialism is, why it happened, and why it cannot be justified.

Definition of Colonialism

1. What is colonialism? How is it harmful for colonized countries?
• Define colonialism / How would you describe colonialism?• What negative effects did colonialism have on less powerful countries?
Colonialism is the practice by which a powerful country directly controls less powerful countries and uses their resources to increase its own power and wealth.

It was harmful because colonized countries lost control of their resources, land, governments, and cultures. Their wealth was extracted to benefit the colonizing power, leaving local populations poor and powerless.
2. How is colonialism different from simple trade between countries?
• What makes colonialism more than just trade?
Trade involves a willing exchange of goods. Colonialism goes further — it involves one country forcibly taking direct political and military control over another country and exploiting its resources and people without fair exchange.
3. Name all the major causes of colonialism.
• List the reasons why European countries colonized Asian and African nations• What were the economic causes of colonialism?
The major causes of colonialism were:

1. Economic gain: To bring wealth from colonies through trade and resources.
2. New sea routes: Explorers like Vasco da Gama and Columbus opened paths to Asia and Africa.
3. Unstable political conditions: Weak governments in Asia and Africa invited European intervention.
4. Civilizing mission: Europeans claimed to spread religion and culture (used as an excuse).
5. Raw materials: Needed cheaply for growing European industries.
6. New markets: To sell European manufactured goods to large colonial populations.
4. Why did the 'civilizing mission' NOT justify colonialism?
• What was wrong with the 'civilizing mission' as a reason for colonialism?
The 'civilizing mission' was used as an excuse to justify colonialism, not as a genuine attempt to help. Europeans did not treat colonized peoples as equals — they exploited resources, suppressed cultures and religions, and used force to control them. It was about economic and political gain, not genuine help.
Part 2

European Powers in the Subcontinent

Portuguese, Dutch, French, British — from first contact to the Battle of Wandiwash.

Portuguese and British in India

1. Who were the first Europeans to arrive in the Indian subcontinent?
• Who was Vasco da Gama? When did he arrive in India?• Name the earliest European traders to reach South Asia
The first Europeans were Portuguese sailors. Vasco da Gama, a famous Portuguese explorer, arrived in Calicut in 1495 and was astonished by the prosperity and abundance of resources in India.

At first, the Portuguese traded peacefully. Later they adopted harsher policies and for almost a hundred years maintained themselves as the sole traders in India, making Portugal very wealthy. Their power declined by the end of the 16th century as the Dutch and British began to compete.
2. What was the East India Company? Which major ports did the British set up?
• Who established the East India Company?• Name the three cities where the British established major trading ports
The East India Company (EIC) was a trading company established by the merchants of London. It started trade with India and eventually became one of the most powerful forces in the subcontinent.

The British established major ports in Madras, Surat, and Bombay.
3. What was the Battle of Wandiwash (1760)? Why is it a turning point?
• Who were the two main contestants trading in India by the 1750s?• What was the significance of the Battle of Wandiwash?
By the 1750s, the British and French were the two main contestants in India. The Battle of Wandiwash was fought in 1760 between France and Britain. Britain won and gained almost complete control over the Indian subcontinent, effectively ending French power in India.

It was a turning point because before 1760 both Britain and France were powerful rivals. After Wandiwash, Britain had no major European rival left to challenge their dominance.
Part 3

Invasions That Weakened the Mughal Empire

Nadir Shah, Ahmad Shah Abdali, and their impact on Mughal authority.

Nadir Shah and Ahmad Shah Abdali

1. Who was Nadir Shah? What happened at the Battle of Karnal (1739)?
• When did Nadir Shah invade India and what was the result?• What did Nadir Shah take from Delhi after his invasion?
Nadir Shah's full name was Nadir Quli Khan. In 1736, he overthrew the Safavid Dynasty of Persia and took the title of Nadir Shah. He was attracted to the subcontinent because of the weak government of the later Mughal rulers.

In 1739, Nadir Shah invaded India and defeated the Mughals easily in the Battle of Karnal. He captured Delhi, thousands were killed, and he took the renowned Peacock Throne of Shah Jahan. His invasion shattered the central authority of the Mughals permanently.
2. Who was Ahmad Shah Abdali? How did his invasions affect the subcontinent?
• Which region did Ahmad Shah Abdali frequently invade?• Why did Ahmad Shah Abdali's invasions further weaken the Mughal Empire?
Ahmad Shah Abdali was a young and talented commander of Nadir Shah. He accompanied Nadir Shah to Delhi in 1739 and later frequently invaded Punjab to expand his power in Afghanistan.

His repeated invasions drained resources, disrupted governance, and further undermined Mughal authority — making it easier for the British to eventually take control.
Part 4

Key Battles and EIC Control

Battle of Plassey, Buxar, Third Battle of Panipat, and the takeover of Delhi.

Battle of Plassey (1757)

1. What was the Battle of Plassey? Who were the parties and who won?
• Who was Mir Jafar? What role did he play in the Battle of Plassey?• What caused the Battle of Plassey?
The Battle of Plassey was fought in 1757 between the EIC and Nawab Siraj-ud-Daula of Bengal.

In 1756, Siraj-ud-Daula — encouraged by the French — attacked the EIC base in Calcutta. In response, Sir Robert Clive led EIC soldiers to fight back.

Mir Jafar, Siraj-ud-Daula's own general, betrayed him by helping Robert Clive achieve victory. This treachery led directly to the defeat and death of Siraj-ud-Daula. Mir Jafar was rewarded by being made the new Nawab of Bengal.
📝 The British won largely through internal betrayal, not purely military strength — a key point for analysis questions.
2. What was the Battle of Buxar (1764)? Why was it more significant than Plassey?
• Name the three rulers who joined forces against the EIC in the Battle of Buxar• What territories came under British control after the Battle of Buxar?
The Battle of Buxar was fought in 1764 between the EIC and the combined forces of: (1) Mir Qasim (Nawab of Bengal), (2) Shuja-ud-Daula (Nawab of Awadh), and (3) Shah Alam II (Mughal Emperor). The British emerged victorious and solidified their control over Orissa, Bengal, and Bihar.

Buxar was more significant than Plassey because at Plassey the British won through betrayal. At Buxar, the British defeated a much larger combined alliance in a real military confrontation — proving their power was undeniable.
3. What was the Third Battle of Panipat (1761)? How did it benefit the British?
• Who was Shah Walliullah? What did he request Ahmad Shah Abdali to do?• What was the result of the Third Battle of Panipat?
The Third Battle of Panipat was fought in 1761 between the Marathas and the army of Ahmad Shah Abdali. Shah Walliullah, a Muslim scholar, had requested Abdali to come restore Muslim rule. The Marathas suffered a crushing defeat and were divided into smaller states that fought amongst each other.

This indirectly benefited the British because the divided, weakened Marathas could not unite against them. When the British fought the Marathas later, they faced fragmented forces — making the British takeover of Delhi in 1803 much easier.
4. How did the British take over Delhi in 1803?
• What was the Battle of Delhi at Patparganj?• Why could the Mughal Emperor not resist British advances in Delhi?
In the early 19th century, Delhi was under Maratha control. The 1803 Battle of Delhi at Patparganj was fought by the British under General Louis Bourquin, who drove the Marathas towards the Yamuna River. The Marathas were defeated and the British entered Delhi.

The Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II was forced to accept 'British Protection' — demonstrating the complete weakness of the Mughals and the undeniable dominance of the British.

Resistance in the Deccan — Haider Ali and Tipu Sultan

1. Who was Haider Ali? Which kingdom offered the strongest resistance to the British?
• How did Haider Ali rise to become the ruler of Mysore?• How many wars did Haider Ali and Tipu Sultan fight against the British?
The Kingdom of Mysore was the stronghold of resistance to the British in the Deccan. Haider Ali rose from army officer to commander of the army through his military skill. Upon the death of the Raja, he assumed absolute command over Mysore.

Haider Ali and his son and successor Tipu Sultan together engaged with British forces in four Anglo-Mysore Wars, each lasting multiple years — making them the most formidable opponents of British expansion in the Deccan.
